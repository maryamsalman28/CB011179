TURTLE CATEGORIES

Green Sea Turtle

Second in size only to leatherbacks is the green sea turtle. They can grow to a length of four feet and a weight of up to 500 pounds (225 kg). The adult consumes seagrasses, seaweeds, algae, and other types of marine plant life as a herbivore. Their sharp, highly serrated beak is ideal for scraping algae off of hard surfaces and for feeding in seagrass beds.


Olive Ridley Sea Turtle

The olive ridley turtle is one of the most common kinds of sea turtle in the world and is well-known for its large nesting groups, or arribadas. As one of the smallest sea turtle species, olive ridley turtles are just 2 to 2.5 feet (0.6 to 0.7 m) long and weigh 80 to 110 pounds (36 to 50 kg). They got their name from the olive hue of their shells, which have a heart-like form.


Leatherback Sea Turtle

The biggest turtle in the world is the leatherback sea turtle. Only this particular kind of sea turtle has a soft shell and no scales. They have been there since the time of the dinosaurs and get their name from their tough, rubbery skin. Highly migratory, leatherbacks can travel over 10,000 kilometres annually between their breeding and feeding areas.


Hawksbill Sea Turtle

All of the world's main seas have tropical and subtropical waters where hawksbill sea turtles can be found. The term "hawksbill" refers to the bird's unusual beak-like mouth, which is ideal for locating food sources in difficult-to-reach cracks and crevices. They are the only type of sea turtle that can subsist primarily on sponges as food. The functioning of marine ecosystems depends on them.
